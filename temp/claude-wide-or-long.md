# Wide, long, or invariant: picking the right schema for 1v1 RTS prediction

**The evidence converges on a clear recommendation: represent every match as two symmetric rows (`player_side`/`opponent_side`), train on differenced or set-based features, and enforce swap invariance in the model – either architecturally (Bradley‑Terry/Siamese/GNN) or through swap augmentation at train and average-of-two-predictions at inference.** This is the pattern that has won the Kaggle NCAA March Madness tournament year after year, is implicit in every well-behaved Elo/Glicko/TrueSkill rating system, and is the only formulation that simultaneously (a) kills the most common leakage bug in pairwise datasets, (b) remains compatible with tree boosters, linear models, neural networks, and graph neural networks, and (c) maps cleanly onto the Bradley–Terry likelihood that underlies most of the relevant literature. For a master's thesis comparing several model families on StarCraft II and Age of Empires II, the design question is less "wide vs. long" than "how do I enforce the symmetry P(A beats B) = 1 − P(B beats A) while keeping a single reproducible feature store?" – and the answer is a long "player-perspective" table from which any model-specific view (wide-differenced, Siamese pair, set, graph) can be materialised deterministically.

The rest of this report develops that conclusion, reviews the literature that supports it, highlights pitfalls that have tripped up published RTS/esports work, and offers a concrete Python/DuckDB/pandas pipeline and evaluation methodology suitable for a thesis.

## What the literature actually does

Pre-game outcome prediction for RTS specifically is **under-studied compared to in-game prediction**. Most StarCraft II win-prediction papers (Synnaeve & Bessière 2011; Erickson & Buro 2014; Ravari et al.; Kim et al. 2022 "3-D ResNet") predict from replay states *during* a match, not from pre-match player features. The AoE2 literature is even sparser: the IEEE 2023 paper by Adınır & colleagues ("Regression Analysis of Age of Empires II DE Match Results with Machine Learning") and a widely-read Medium write-up by Mike Xie are the two most relevant references, and Xie's post is the clearest published statement of the exact schema problem you are asking about.

Xie's pipeline starts with **one row per player**, which is the raw form Voobly/AoE2.net export. He explicitly reports the leakage trap: "The data originally had one player per row. I had to shuffle the rows randomly since the odd number players were always the match-winner which caused leakage in the models at first. And then merge the odd and even rows so each row was both players in the match and make new features like their difference in Elo points and civilization strength." This one paragraph captures two of the three most important lessons in the field: **(1) a raw long table where "row 1 = winner, row 2 = loser" leaks the label via row order, and (2) differenced features (Elo_A − Elo_B, civ_tier_A − civ_tier_B) are the natural symmetric encoding** for tree-based and linear models. Xie's XGBoost reached ~77% accuracy using Elo-difference and civ-tier-difference as the dominant features, with tree models substantially beating logistic regression – a pattern that recurs in Kaggle.

On the **Kaggle NCAA March Madness** competition (running since 2014, now at a $50K prize pool), the canonical "raddar" solution – documented in public notebooks, reproduced by dozens of top finishers, and validated in a 2023 *American Statistician* paper on "Conformal Win Probability" – prescribes exactly the schema a 1v1 RTS thesis needs. Raddar **doubles every game: once as (T1=A, T2=B) with label 1 and once as (T1=B, T2=A) with label 0**, uses `T1_*`/`T2_*` columns, trains XGBoost on point-differential (regression), then maps predicted margin to probability via a spline calibration. This doubling is the Kaggle-proven answer to swap invariance: it turns "one row per match" into "two rows per match" and forces the model to see both orderings. Top solutions consistently use **difference features** (seed-diff, Elo-diff, AdjOE-diff, KenPom-diff, FG%-diff), ensemble logistic regression with XGBoost/LightGBM, and **clip predictions to roughly [0.025, 0.975]** to cap Brier/log-loss penalties on confidently-wrong predictions. Men's and women's tournaments are trained separately; combining them degrades performance.

The **sports analytics** tradition arrives at the same answer from a different direction. Kovalchik's 2016 survey of tennis models ("Searching for the GOAT of Tennis Win Prediction") shows that **FiveThirtyEight's Elo-based logistic model outperforms both Bradley-Terry and point-based stochastic models** on calibration and log-loss, which is important because Elo is just a one-dimensional player embedding updated online via the Bradley-Terry likelihood – i.e. the simplest possible antisymmetric model f(A,B) = σ(r_A − r_B). Sipko's 2015 Imperial thesis engineered 22 features (fatigue, H2H, time-discounted form, surface-weighted rates, common-opponent adjusted win rates) and found an ANN with these features achieved a 4.35% betting ROI – a 75% improvement over the previous Common-Opponent state of the art. Every one of Sipko's features is defined per-player and then entered into the model **as a difference or concatenation**; none is defined on the match level directly. Kovalchik's 2020 margin-of-victory Elo and Angelini/Candila/De Angelis's 2022 weighted Elo (EJOR 297:120-132) both extend the same rating-as-antisymmetric-score idea. Glickman's Glicko/Glicko-2 adds a rating-deviation parameter; Herbrich, Minka & Graepel's **TrueSkill** (NeurIPS 2007) places a Gaussian on skill and does message-passing inference; Coulom's **Whole-History Rating** (2008) fits an exact MAP under the dynamic Bradley-Terry likelihood and beats Elo, Glicko, and TrueSkill on Go. For a thesis, these are not competitors to your ML models – they are **feature generators** whose outputs (rating, uncertainty, volatility) become the most predictive single columns in any downstream classifier.

The **Bradley-Terry model** is the mathematical spine that ties all of this together. Its log-odds parameterisation, logit P(i > j) = β_i − β_j, is literally antisymmetric: it guarantees P(A beats B) = 1 − P(B beats A) by construction. Király & Qian's 2017 arXiv 1701.08055 "Bradley-Terry-Élő" paper reframes Elo as online stochastic-gradient MLE under Bradley-Terry and introduces "structured log-odds models" that embed player features – this is effectively a linear neural Bradley-Terry and should be a baseline in your thesis. Fujii's 2023 **Neural Bradley-Terry Rating** puts both players through a shared-weight deep network to produce a scalar strength β_player(features), then predicts σ(β_A − β_B); it is the direct generalisation of Elo to deep learning. The fact that the Bradley-Terry model is now the cornerstone of RLHF reward modelling in LLMs (Christiano et al. 2017; Ouyang et al. 2022) means there is active and well-maintained open-source tooling for fitting such models at scale.

## The data-representation question, resolved

**Wide (1 row per match) and long (2 rows per match) are equivalent representations of the same information – but they are not equivalent as training regimes.** A 1-row-per-match wide table with `p1_*`/`p2_*` columns and a label "p1 wins" is asymmetric by construction: unless every p1 column appears identically in a paired swap, a gradient-boosted tree will learn spurious structure from the ordering convention (e.g., whoever happens to be alphabetically first, or whoever the replay parser listed first). A raw long table with "row 1 = winner, row 2 = loser" leaks the label as row position – exactly Xie's AoE2 bug. The three legitimate options are:

**Option 1 – Long "player-perspective" table with swap augmentation (recommended primary representation).** Each match generates two rows: `(player_side_id, opponent_side_id, player_features, opponent_features, label=1 if player won)`. Before training, shuffle within match pairs to remove any residual order signal. This is Raddar's NCAA approach translated to 1v1. It doubles the row count but doubles information content too, because the model sees each matchup from both sides; it trivially guarantees that the model class has access to the symmetric loss structure. For inference, **predict once and use that probability, or predict both (A,B) and (B,A) and average** – the latter is a useful sanity check for models that are not architecturally symmetric, because the gap |p_AB − (1 − p_BA)| is a direct measure of residual asymmetry.

**Option 2 – Wide table with differenced/symmetric encodings.** Collapse per-player features into symmetric operators: difference (Elo_A − Elo_B), absolute difference (|winrate_A − winrate_B|), min/max, ratio, and – for categorical features like race/civ – a *pair* key (e.g. (Terran, Zerg) → TvZ) or one-hot difference vectors. This is the Sipko, FiveThirtyEight, and 2026 Marcu-Kaggle formulation. Difference features are **intrinsically antisymmetric** for linear models (sign-flipping the difference flips the logit, giving P(A|diff) = 1 − P(B|-diff)), which for a linear or monotonic-in-one-variable model recovers swap invariance for free without row duplication. Trees are not linear but gradient boosters respect sign symmetry empirically well in this setting because difference features are signed scalars with a natural zero point.

**Option 3 – Set/graph representation.** The match is a set {A, B} or a graph edge between two player-nodes with side-specific edge features (chosen race, map, tournament). This is the formulation used by Xenopoulos & Silva ("Graph Neural Networks to Predict Sports Outcomes", 2021 IEEE Big Data / arXiv 2207.14124), who report 20% lower test loss than state of the art on an esports dataset, and by Rahimian's SFU thesis "Sports Match Outcome Prediction with Graph Representation Learning" which compares SimpleGNN, SportsNet (a heterogeneous team-player graph with Blade-Chest decoder), and Elo. Deep Sets (Zaheer et al., NeurIPS 2017) proves that any permutation-invariant function of a set can be written as ρ(∑φ(x_i)); applied to two players this reduces to a pooled two-player set and handles the symmetry by construction. Set Transformer (Lee et al. 2019) generalises this with attention.

The tradeoffs are summarised in the following table.

| Representation | Swap invariance | Leakage risk | Feature engineering | Best model families | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wide, raw p1_*/p2_* | **Violated** | Low if labelled cleanly; high if row order encodes winner | Easiest | None – all models learn spurious order signal | – |
| Wide, differenced/symmetric | Guaranteed for linear; near-guaranteed for GBM | Low | Moderate (design symmetric operators per feature) | Logistic regression, XGBoost, LightGBM, MLPs | Small |
| Long (2 rows/match) + swap aug | Guaranteed empirically via training | **High if not split carefully** – both rows of a match must be in same fold | Simple (features are "player" and "opponent") | All, especially neural | 2× rows |
| Siamese / shared-encoder NN | Guaranteed architecturally | Low | Moderate (build a per-player tower) | Neural networks | Shared weights |
| Antisymmetric head (Bradley-Terry): f = g(A) − g(B) | **Guaranteed exactly** via construction | Low | Simple | Neural, linear, trees (on g output) | Need per-player scoring function |
| Deep Set / Set Transformer | Guaranteed architecturally | Low | Simple | Neural networks | Pooling layer |
| Graph (player nodes + edges) | Guaranteed | Low | High (graph construction, temporal snapshots) | GNNs, R-GCN, TGN, GraphSAGE | Graph infra |

**For a thesis comparing multiple model families the principled choice is to maintain the long "player-perspective" table as the single source of truth and derive every other view from it deterministically.** From that table, a wide-differenced frame for XGBoost is a single `GROUP BY match_id` with pivots; a Siamese training batch is a pair of side-aligned tensors; a graph dataset is a straightforward edge list with timestamped node features. This keeps the feature store symmetrical and reproducible.

## How to build per-player features without leaking the future

**Temporal leakage is the single biggest failure mode in this problem**, and the failure mode that is most likely to slip through peer review if your metrics look unusually strong. Every per-player aggregate (MMR, win rate over last N games, form on map X, matchup win rate vs race R, head-to-head record vs opponent) must be computed **strictly from matches played before the match being predicted**, using an as-of join on match timestamp. Expanding means, rolling windows of last N matches, exponentially-decayed averages, and time-discounted head-to-head counters are all appropriate; simple season-level aggregates are almost always leaky because they include the match being predicted.

In DuckDB and pandas this is best implemented with an **as-of join**: for each (match_id, player_id) row in the long table, join to the player's history table on `history.timestamp < match.timestamp` and aggregate with window functions. DuckDB supports `ASOF JOIN` natively; in pandas the equivalent is `pd.merge_asof(direction='backward')`. Compute every rolling/expanding feature this way, including Elo-style ratings: update Elo sequentially in chronological order and store the **pre-match rating** as the feature, never the post-match rating. For TrueSkill and Glicko use the TrueSkill/`trueskill` and `glicko2` Python packages with a strict chronological pass over the data. Matchup-specific features (player A's historical win rate vs Zerg, vs opponent B specifically) require grouped as-of joins; make sure the grouping keys (race, civ, opponent_id, map) do not themselves depend on future information.

**Train/test splits must be temporal**, not random. Split by match date – everything before date D is train, everything after is test – and use expanding-window or rolling walk-forward cross-validation for model selection. Buhamra, Groll, Ley, and colleagues (2024/2025) use expanding-window CV for tennis Grand Slam prediction and this is now the reference methodology in statistically-enhanced learning for sport. When you duplicate rows for swap augmentation, **the two rows of the same match must land in the same fold** – either use `GroupKFold` on match_id plus a time constraint, or duplicate rows *after* splitting. Violating this rule gives grossly optimistic scores because the model effectively sees the same match in train and test.

A further leakage trap specific to esports is the **patch / meta shift**: civs and races get rebalanced, new expansions change winrates dramatically. Always include a patch/version indicator as a feature, and for the final out-of-sample evaluation use matches from the most recent patch only. This is analogous to the men's/women's tournament separation in NCAA Kaggle and to the surface-specific modelling in tennis.

## Model-family-specific guidance

**Logistic regression and GLMs**: use the wide-differenced representation. Your coefficients on Elo-diff, winrate-diff, matchup-diff, and one-hot civ-pair variables are directly interpretable and the model satisfies swap invariance by construction when all features are differences. This is a strong baseline that will beat flashier methods on small data.

**Gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost)**: use the long format with swap augmentation *and* a wide-differenced view. In practice the raddar pattern – train regression on margin of victory where available, then map to probability via isotonic or spline calibration – dominates plain binary classification in NCAA competitions. For AoE2 and SC2 you probably don't have a good "margin of victory" (games are binary), so train binary classification with logloss and calibrate with Platt or isotonic on a held-out temporal validation set. Clip predictions.

**Siamese / twin networks**: build a shared MLP that ingests a single player's feature vector and produces an embedding, then combine the two embeddings by concatenation + MLP head, or preferably as a **Bradley-Terry head**: logit = g(A) − g(B) with a shared scalar output g. The BT head is exactly antisymmetric, requires no swap augmentation, and empirically trains well. Include contextual features (map, patch, game mode) either as conditioning on g (g(A; map)) or as match-level features post-concatenation. Fujii's Neural Bradley-Terry paper is the direct reference.

**Graph neural networks**: construct a heterogeneous graph with player nodes, race/civ nodes, and map nodes, with edges encoding historical matches (player-player, player-race, player-map). Node features are the engineered per-player features; edge features include timestamp, patch, outcome. Formulate prediction as a **link prediction problem**: given a new edge between players A and B at time t, predict the probability that the edge is labelled "A wins". GraphSAGE or R-GCN (for heterogeneous edges) is a reasonable starting architecture; TGN or TGAT adds explicit temporal handling. Xenopoulos & Silva's arXiv 2207.14124 and Rahimian's SFU thesis are the two closest references. The GNN handles swap invariance naturally because edges are unordered; the direction of prediction is supplied by a side feature ("which node's perspective are we predicting?"). GNNs will be strongest on pro/semi-pro tournament data where the player-player graph has meaningful structure; on random-matchmaking ladder data the graph is almost a tree and GNNs will underperform simpler models.

**Learned player embeddings**: two routes. (1) **Implicit via Bradley-Terry**: fit a plain logistic regression with one-hot player identity on both sides and read off the coefficients – these are log-strengths and are exactly Elo. Add a low-rank factorisation for matchup-specific effects (cf. chess "blade-chest" models and the Team/Player Blade-Chest Model in Rahimian's thesis). (2) **Explicit via neural embedding**: treat player IDs as tokens, train an embedding layer end-to-end inside the Siamese/BT architecture. Analogues from MOBAs – hero2vec, champion2vec, and the ZenML Dota draft-prediction blog using Word2Vec-style hero embeddings followed by mean pooling – show this works with surprisingly little data. In AoE2 you can embed both player IDs and civilisations; in SC2 embed player IDs and races. Embeddings also unlock the **cross-game transfer** secondary goal: freeze the non-ID architecture, retrain only embeddings, or use a multi-task head.

## Cross-game transfer between SC2 and AoE2

For transfer learning across SC2 and AoE2, the long schema already pays dividends: if your player-perspective table has columns `game, player_id, opponent_id, player_side_rating, opponent_side_rating, player_form, opponent_form, pick_embedding, opponent_pick_embedding, map_embedding, label`, a model trained on one game can be fine-tuned on the other by re-learning only the embedding tables (race/civ/map/player-id) while keeping the skill-and-form portion of the network shared. This is analogous to domain adaptation in multi-task learning; Müller et al.'s and Vaswani et al.'s multi-head transformer ideas translate well. Report transfer results both directions (SC2 → AoE2, AoE2 → SC2) and include a "no transfer" baseline trained only on the target game – without that baseline a transfer result means nothing.

## A concrete Python/DuckDB/pandas pipeline

Build a single canonical **long `matches_long` table** in DuckDB with one row per `(match_id, player_side)` – so two rows per match – including timestamp, player_id, opponent_id, chosen_race_or_civ, opponent_race_or_civ, map_id, patch, tournament_id, and the raw outcome. All feature engineering happens via DuckDB SQL `ASOF JOIN` against a `player_history` table so that every feature is pre-match. Downstream materialisations:

- `feat_wide_diff`: `SELECT match_id, f1.player_id AS p1, f2.player_id AS p2, f1.elo - f2.elo AS elo_diff, ..., label FROM matches_long f1 JOIN matches_long f2 USING (match_id) WHERE f1.side < f2.side` – gives 1 row per match with difference columns, suitable for logistic regression and XGBoost.
- `feat_long_symmetric`: the raw long table itself, shuffled and split temporally, suitable for neural models with Bradley-Terry heads or for the "predict both orderings, average" pattern.
- `feat_graph`: an edge list plus timestamped node-feature snapshots, suitable for PyG or DGL GNNs.

Use `sklearn`'s `TimeSeriesSplit` (or a manual temporal group split) for cross-validation, log everything with MLflow or Weights & Biases, and include a **symmetry audit**: for each model, predict every test match in both orderings and report the mean and 95th percentile of |p_AB + p_BA − 1|. For BT-head, Siamese, and Deep-Set models this should be machine zero; for tree boosters trained with swap augmentation it should be under 0.01; anything larger indicates a training bug. Publish the audit in an appendix.

For reproducibility, pin everything with `uv` or `poetry`, seed all RNGs, commit the DuckDB export of the canonical long table as a versioned artefact, and record the git SHA, data snapshot hash, and model hyperparameters for every run. A thesis defence that demonstrates bit-identical re-running of all results on a fresh clone is the strongest possible evidence of methodological care.

## Methodology and evaluation for a master's thesis

Report **Brier score, log-loss, and calibration (reliability diagrams, Expected Calibration Error)** alongside accuracy and AUC. Accuracy alone is misleading because 1v1 outcomes are well approximated by a coin flip near equal skill; calibrated probabilities matter much more for any practical use and are the standard Kaggle metric. Include a **skill-stratified evaluation**: accuracy at equal-Elo, at 100-Elo gap, at 300-Elo gap. Models often look good overall only because they handle lopsided matchups, which Elo alone predicts well; the real value-add of ML is accuracy in close matches. Compare against three principled baselines: (1) pure Elo pre-match, (2) pure TrueSkill or Glicko pre-match, (3) logistic regression on Elo-diff plus civ/race pair. If your XGBoost or GNN cannot beat baseline (3) in log-loss on held-out recent matches, you have no story; if it beats them by more than a few percent you have a real finding worth defending.

Discuss explicitly the threats to validity: smurfing (AoE2's untracked alt accounts, documented by Xie), patch shifts, pro vs ladder data distribution shifts, and survivorship bias (high-rated players have many more matches so dominate loss). Where applicable, use **inverse-propensity weighting by player match count** or subsample to equalise representation; report both weighted and unweighted metrics.

## Conclusion

Across twenty years of rating theory, a decade of Kaggle pairwise competitions, and the emerging GNN-for-sports literature, the same architectural choice keeps winning: a **symmetric representation, enforced either by data (swap-doubled long rows with temporal as-of features) or by architecture (Bradley-Terry / Siamese / Deep-Set / GNN heads)**. The Kaggle raddar pattern – double the rows, difference the features, train XGBoost on margin, calibrate, clip – is the battle-tested default; the Neural Bradley-Terry head is the cleanest neural generalisation and the only formulation that makes P(A beats B) = 1 − P(B beats A) a theorem rather than an empirical hope.

For the specific thesis you describe, the recommended plan is concise. Keep one canonical long `matches_long` table in DuckDB, computed with as-of joins so no feature leaks the future. From it materialise a wide-differenced view for logistic regression and XGBoost, a Siamese/BT training loader for neural models, and a heterogeneous player-race-map graph for GNN experiments. Baselines are pure Elo, pure TrueSkill, and logistic regression on Elo-diff plus civ-pair; challenger models are XGBoost on differenced features, a Neural Bradley-Terry model with shared MLP and player/civ embeddings, and an R-GCN link-prediction model over the player graph. Evaluate with log-loss, Brier, and calibration diagrams, stratified by Elo gap, using strict temporal splits and a swap-symmetry audit. That design will give you defensible empirical comparisons across model families, a principled answer to the data-layout question, and – via the embedding layers – a clean path to the SC2 ↔ AoE2 transfer experiments. The novelty lies less in any single technique than in the rigorous comparison itself: such a comparison has not yet been published for 1v1 RTS pre-game prediction, which is exactly the gap the thesis fills.
